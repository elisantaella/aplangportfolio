College Essay

UC Personal Insight Question #3: What would you say is your greatest talent or skill? How have you developed and demonstrated that talent over time?

The stadium lighting came to life as I tweaked the crossfader, my hands moving swiftly between the turntables as 3,000 people held their breath for the beat to kick in. I wasn't Eli Santaella, Beverly Hills High student, in that instant—instead, I was the conductor of a symphony of sound, code, and motion. My greatest strength is not in a single aptitude; it is in how I reconcile multiplicity to create harmony. Just as a DJ can seamlessly transition between tracks, I've learned to merge and mix my Puerto Rican and Persian heritage, soccer and coding, music production and academics, and this synthesis talent is my superpower. It began at the age of eight, with me being diagnosed with the same ADHD as my dad. Others perceived it as a limitation, but I realized that it was how my brain processed many streams of information at once. I took up coding at the age of ten, not in spite of the ADHD, but because of it—my brain needed the steady stimulation of debugging, problem-solving, and building. By the age of thirteen, I was creating electronic music, mixing tracks the way I mixed functions in programs. On the pitch, this multidimensional thinking made me a midfielder who read the game like sheet music. I notice patterns where other people notice disorder—same pattern recognition that allows me to debug code or transition seamlessly between songs. When I learned that my mother suffered from an autoimmune disease, I used this ability to design a health-monitoring app for her, marrying technology with profound empathy. Last year, I put on "Beats for a Cause," a fundraising event that paired a soccer tournament with a music festival from which we raised $15,000 for autoimmune disease research. I programmed the registration system, DJ'd the afterparty, and competed in the tournament. I learned that day that my biggest talent isn't multi-passionate so much as it is being able to make people see that we don't ever have to pick only one part of ourselves. I am not applying to this university to become a specialist in only computer science, or only in music, or only in athletics. I am looking for a place where I can further hone this talent for synthesis, where interdisciplinary thinking is not only tolerated but admired. Because in a world that is increasingly calling for innovation, my skill at bridging unrelated dots—to hear the beat between code and afrohouse, between algorithms and adagios—is not only my talent. It's the contribution I will make to the future.

5. Class Sizes

Intro classes: 100-200 students with smaller discussion sections (15-20). Upper-level CS courses: 20-50 students. Seminars: 5-15 students.

6. Admissions Process

Holistic review process with 3.96% acceptance rate. Offers Restrictive Early Action (November 1) and Regular Decision (January 5). The Mayfield Fellows Program provides honors opportunities for tech entrepreneurship.

7. Cost & Financial Aid

  • Total cost: ~$82,000/year
  • Need-blind admissions with 100% demonstrated need met
  • Families earning under $100k pay no tuition
  • Athletic scholarships available for Division I soccer
  • Numerous CS-specific scholarships and paid research opportunities

8. What's Most Appealing

The intersection of elite academics and Division I athletics, combined with a culture that celebrates "nerd-athletes" like me. The proximity to tech innovation, the collaborative rather than competitive atmosphere, and the opportunity to work with professors who are literally inventing the future of AI and computer science.

9. Fun Fact

Stanford's "Stanford Laptop Orchestra" (SLOrk) uses laptops as musical instruments, combining my love for coding and music. Also, the campus has a particle accelerator that runs underneath the quad!

10. Why I Selected Stanford

Stanford embodies everything I aspire to be: technically innovative, athletically competitive, and culturally diverse. It's where I can play Division I soccer while developing AI that composes music, where my ADHD hyperfocus becomes a superpower in late-night coding sessions, and where being a Persian-Puerto Rican tech enthusiast who DJs on weekends isn't unique—it's celebrated. Stanford isn't just a school; it's where multifaceted students like me transform into the innovators who will shape tomorrow's world.

Reflections

B. Kind Gesture

The fluorescent tubes in the LA Public Library's computer lab reflect off the frustrated look on the 70-year-old veteran's face as he struggles to video call his grandchildren for the first time. I was visiting for my weekly tutoring session, but his perseverance in the face of confusion brought to mind the way my own mother struggled using her health portal with shaking fingertips. "Those damn machines," he growled, clicking aimlessly at the screen. I pulled over a chair next to him, and over the course of the next two hours, we navigated the video calling basics together. It was more than a lesson in technology, though. When I taught him to unmute his mic, he spoke to me of his grandkids in Texas. When we sorted out his camera settings, he spoke of his time in Vietnam and returning to a world that was already gone—a sense in which he said technology often caught him up. The second his granddaughter's face was on screen and her eyes lighted up at seeing "Grandpa!", I was filled with a sense of joy that was impossible to match with coding achievements. Tears came to Marcus's eyes as she pulled out her drawings to share with him, and he kept repeating, "I can see you, baby girl. I can really see you." That day changed the way I look at technology. It's not the beauty of the code or the creativity of the algorithm—it's the connection. Each week since, Marcus comes in with new questions, and I've seen him go from being afraid of the computer to sending daily photos to his family WhatsApp group. He eveninquired me to DJ his playlist for the dance at the veterans' center, laughing that perhaps we can "drop some beats together." I learned from this experience that the biggest kindness is not grand actions—it is patience, being there, and sharing with others without the judgment. In a world hurrying to the next breakthrough, the most revolutionary action is sometimes slowing down to make sure no one is left behind. Marcus refers to me as his "tech grandson," and the truth is, I've learned more from him on the topic of resilient adaptation than I would from a coding tutorial. Real innovation is not only pushing the boundaries but is bringing everyone with you on the ride.

C. Current Event - The LA Wildfires: When Code Meets Crisis

The orange glow wasn't from sunset—it was 2 PM on a Tuesday in October 2024, and Los Angeles was burning again. The Palisades Fire had erupted just miles from my home, painting our Beverly Hills sky in apocalyptic hues. But this time was different. This time, I watched my community respond not just with evacuation orders and emergency services, but with technology that quite literally saved lives. What struck me most was seeing my own neighbors—many of them tech workers—rapidly deploy solutions. Within hours, a UCLA computer science student had created a real-time evacuation route optimizer that considered traffic patterns and fire spread. Local developers built a neighborhood communication app that helped elderly residents find rides to safety. Even my high school coding club pivoted our meeting to create a simple website aggregating shelter information in multiple languages, recognizing our diverse LA community. The intersection of climate crisis and technological response revealed something profound about our generation. We're not just digital natives—we're crisis innovators. While news outlets focused on destruction, I witnessed creation: makeshift mesh networks when cell towers failed, drone pilots mapping fire lines for firefighters, and AI models predicting wind pattern changes. My family's experience personalized this further. My mother's compromised immune system meant wildfire smoke was especially dangerous. Using air quality APIs, I built a simple alert system that would ping our phones when particulate levels spiked, giving us crucial extra minutes to seal windows and adjust her medications. It wasn't sophisticated code, but it was necessary code. The fires eventually contained, leaving 15,000 acres scarred but our community stronger. What lingered wasn't just the acrid smell of smoke, but a new understanding: climate change isn't a future problem—it's a present reality requiring present solutions. As I prepare for college, I carry this lesson with me. The next generation of technologists won't have the luxury of building in isolation. We must code with conscience, innovate with urgency, and always remember that behind every data point is a human life, a family, a community worth protecting.
